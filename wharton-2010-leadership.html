Core Mindset Shift

WIFM — What's In It For Me?

The single most powerful reframe in this course: every audience member is subconsciously asking "why should I care?" Answer that first — before your data, your slides, your argument.

The Acronym

W
What'sthe value proposition for this specific audience?
I
In It— what does the content contain that serves them?
F
Forthem, not for you — make the listener the hero.
M
Me— the individual in the room, not the group in aggregate.

How to Apply It

Before writing a single word, answer these three questions about your audience:

  • What do they already know / believe?
  • What do they fear losing or hope to gain?
  • What decision or action do I need from them?

The Test: If you can't complete the sentence "After this message, my audience will feel/think/do ___" — you're not ready to present.

"People don't care how much you know until they know how much you care — about them, their problems, and their goals."

Common Mistake

  • Showing off knowledge ≠ communicating
  • Your process is rarely their concern
  • Credentials establish trust; content delivers value
  • Jargon excludes — plain language persuades
  • Less is almost always more

Message Architecture

The Four Dimensions of Every Message

Every high-impact communication decision can be mapped to four dimensions. Miss one, and even brilliant content falls flat.

W
WHY

Why does this message matter? What's the stakes? Establish relevance and urgency before asking for attention. The audience must understand the "so what" before processing the "what."

W
WHAT

What is the core message? One sentence. One idea. One ask. If you can't state it in one sentence, you haven't finished thinking. Clarity is a form of respect for your audience's time.

W
WHERE

Where is the right venue? In-person vs. virtual, formal vs. informal, board room vs. hallway. Medium shapes message. Match the channel to the weight of the content.

W
WHEN & HOW

Timing and tone. When is the audience most receptive? How much detail? What emotional register? Delivery is the last mile — content prepared; delivery executed.

The Pyramid Principle (McKinsey Method)

Structure messages top-down, never bottom-up:

  • Headline: Your conclusion / recommendation first
  • Support: 2–3 key reasons or evidence points
  • Detail: Data and backup — only if needed

Executives read the pyramid from the top. Always give them the option to stop.

Situation–Complication–Resolution (SCR)

A narrative structure that creates urgency naturally:

  • Situation: Shared context — where we are today
  • Complication: The tension — what's at risk or broken
  • Resolution: Your recommendation — what to do next

Use SCR when you need to motivate action, not just inform.

Executive Presence

Speaking & Presenting Skills

What your instructors observed, and what the research confirms: presence is largely a set of trainable behaviors, not a personality trait.

The 3 V's of Communication

55%
Visual (Body Language)

Eye contact, posture, gestures, facial expression. The audience reads your body before your words. Stand still when making key points. Move with intention.

38%
Vocal (Tone & Pace)

Volume, pace, pitch variation, pausing. Monotone = disengaged audience. Pause before key points — silence signals importance. Slow down when it matters.

7%
Verbal (Words)

The actual content of the words. Surprisingly small in isolation — which is why delivery training matters so much. Words only land when they ride the right wave of voice and body.

Delivery Mechanics

  • → Eye contact: 3–5 seconds per person, completing a thought before moving on. Scan the full room, not just the decision-maker.
  • → The pause: Silence is not dead air — it's emphasis. Pause before important statements, not after filler words.
  • → Gestures: Use open palms when sharing ideas. Avoid self-touching (hair, face) — it signals anxiety. Keep gestures above the waist.
  • → Stance: Feet shoulder-width apart, weight even. "Power pose" before entering — not during. Plant yourself during key points.
  • → Voice: End statements on a down-note (authority). Reserve up-notes for questions. Filler words ("um," "like") signal processing time — replace with silence.
✓ What Great Presenters Do
  • Open with a hook — a question, story, or provocation
  • State their one core idea within 60 seconds
  • Use stories and concrete examples, not abstractions
  • Invite the audience into the content (questions, polls)
  • Close with a clear call to action
  • Welcome Q&A as a sign of engagement
  • Practice out loud — not in your head
  • Recover gracefully from mistakes (audience forgets; you remember)
✗ What Kills a Presentation
  • Reading from slides or notes
  • Too much content — "I'll go fast through this"
  • Starting with an apology or self-deprecation
  • Hiding behind the podium or lectern
  • Monotone delivery or flat affect
  • Leaving no time for Q&A
  • Answering questions you weren't asked
  • Saying "I don't have a slide for this" — be ready to go off-script

"Nervousness is energy. Redirect it — don't suppress it. The audience feels your energy; they don't know its source."

Managing Nerves & Building Confidence

🧘

Before You Speak

  • Slow, deep breathing activates the parasympathetic system
  • Warm up your voice privately
  • Walk the space if possible
  • Focus on the audience's needs, not your own performance
🎯

In the Moment

  • Find one friendly face to anchor your opening
  • Slow your pace — nerves speed you up
  • Use purposeful movement to reset
  • Remember: the audience wants you to succeed
📈

Long-Term Growth

  • Seek every opportunity to speak publicly
  • Record yourself and watch it back
  • Request specific feedback (not "how was I?")
  • Competence compounds — comfort follows

Written Communication

Executive Writing Principles

Written messages live longer than spoken ones. They get forwarded, screenshotted, and re-read without context. That demands more precision and intentionality.

The Writing Process

1
Define your purpose and audience first

Who reads this? What do you want them to think, feel, or do? Write this down before opening a blank document.

2
Lead with the headline

Subject lines, email openers, exec summaries — all should state the main point, not tease it. Busy readers skim from the top.

3
Draft without editing — then edit ruthlessly

Separate creation from critique. Get it out first. Then cut by 25–30%. If a sentence doesn't earn its place, delete it.

4
Read it aloud before sending

If you stumble reading it, the reader will stumble too. Awkward phrasing and missing logic surface immediately when spoken.

5
Anticipate questions and objections

A great written piece addresses the reader's "yes, but…" before they raise it. Preempt pushback with evidence and framing.

Slide & Document Design

  • One idea per slide. If you need a second slide, make one.
  • Action title rule: Every slide title should be a declarative sentence that stands alone as a takeaway ("Revenue grew 18% — exceeding plan").
  • Data ≠ insight. Show what the data means, not just what it shows. "Sales are up" vs. "Sales growth confirms the new channel strategy is working."
  • Slides are a leave-behind, not a teleprompter. Your spoken narrative should add to, not repeat, what's on screen.
  • White space is signal, not waste. Crowded slides signal disorganized thinking.
  • The "so what?" test: Point at every element and ask "so what?" If you can't answer, remove it.
Email Best Practices
  • Subject line = your headline. State the action or topic clearly.
  • First sentence = WIFM for the reader.
  • No more than 5 sentences for a simple request.
  • Use bullets for 3+ items — never run them into prose.
  • Put your ask in the last paragraph, not buried in the middle.

Persuasion & Influence

Leading Without Authority

Communication in leadership is rarely top-down command. More often, it's lateral influence — getting peers, stakeholders, and teams to move with you voluntarily.

❤️

Ethos — Credibility

Who you are matters. Establish credibility quickly but not arrogantly. Reference your relevant experience early. Credibility is lent, not assumed.

💡

Logos — Logic

Evidence, data, and reasoning. Structure your argument so it's irrefutable on the facts. But logic alone rarely moves people to action.

🔥

Pathos — Emotion

Story, analogy, and stakes. People decide emotionally and justify rationally. Connect your data to something the audience cares about.

"The most persuasive message isn't the one with the most data — it's the one that makes the listener feel understood before asking them to change."

Cialdini principles, as applied in Wharton module

Handling Difficult Conversations

→
Separate the person from the problem

Attack the issue, not the individual. "The report was late" vs. "You always miss deadlines."

→
Acknowledge before redirecting

Validate the other person's perspective before presenting yours. "I understand why you'd see it that way — here's a different angle."

→
Ask questions before making statements

Curiosity disarms defensiveness. Understanding the other side strengthens your own argument and opens doors to agreement.

The Feedback Loop

Great communicators treat every interaction as a feedback opportunity — both giving and receiving.

  • Giving feedback: Specific + Behavioral + Impact ("When you did X, it caused Y, which matters because Z").
  • Receiving feedback: Listen fully. Don't defend immediately. Say "thank you." Reflect, then decide.
  • Soliciting feedback: Ask specifically — "What was one thing I could have done differently?" not "How did I do?"
  • Closing the loop: Tell people when you act on their feedback. It builds trust and invites more.

  • What landed well?

Pre-Presentation Checklist

  • I can state my single core message in one sentence
  • I know what I want the audience to think / feel / do
  • I've opened with a hook (not "Hi, I'm here to talk about…")
  • Every slide has an action title (declarative sentence)
  • I've practiced out loud at least twice
  • I know my first 30 seconds cold (no notes)
  • I've anticipated the 2–3 hardest questions
  • I know the room, tech, and logistics
  • I have a clear call to action ready for the close
  • I've planned how to handle silence after asking a question
  • I've removed filler words from my opening
  • I know how to gracefully say "I don't know, I'll find out"

Personal Reflections

What I'm Taking Forward

The insights that shifted my perspective — not just what to do, but how I think about communication as a leadership tool.

Biggest Shift

Communication is a Strategy, Not a Skill

I came in thinking this was about polishing delivery. I'm leaving understanding that how I communicate is my leadership strategy. Every message either builds or erodes trust, alignment, and momentum.

Hardest Lesson

The Expert Trap is Real — and I Fall Into It

My instinct is to demonstrate competence by showing my work. But busy leaders don't want to see the work — they want the answer and enough evidence to trust it. I need to start at the conclusion.

Most Actionable

WIFM Before Every Message

This one question — "What's In It For Me (them)?" — will change how I start every email, deck, and conversation. It forces me to think like a listener, not a speaker.

Most Surprising

Silence is the Most Underused Tool

I filled every pause with words. The data says that's a mistake — pauses before key points signal importance. Pauses after asking questions invite genuine response. I'm going to practice uncomfortable silence.
